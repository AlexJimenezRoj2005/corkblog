Second Day in Ireland: What happened on the second day?

Date: March 17, 2025 | Location: Cork, Ireland

The second day after our arrival in Cork coincided with the celebration of Saint Patrick's Day. This was great news for us because, besides having a day off due to the national holiday, we got to experience the local culture and learn a bit more about the city where we’ll be staying for the next three months.

What did we do on the second day?

From the moment we woke up that morning, it was clear that something special was happening. The streets were closed off with fences, music echoed everywhere, and people were dressed in bright green colors with shamrock decorations. You could feel the festive atmosphere all around.

There were people with green wigs, tall green hats, and shirts celebrating Saint Patrick's Day. For us, it all felt extravagant and surreal, as we had never experienced a celebration like this before.

Ireland is famous for its beer culture and brands like Guinness. On Saint Patrick's Day, it seems that people start the party early, with a pint of beer in hand. Around 10:00 a.m., we already saw many locals drinking, which is part of the tradition here.

What is celebrated on Saint Patrick's Day?

Saint Patrick is the patron saint of Ireland, and every year on March 17th, the country celebrates in his honor. The story goes that Patrick was a Christian missionary who arrived in Ireland in the 5th century to spread Christianity. Interestingly, although he is now an Irish symbol, Saint Patrick was not Irish—he was born in Britain.

According to tradition, when he was young, he was captured by pirates and taken to Ireland as a slave. After several years, he managed to escape, but later returned voluntarily to preach Christianity. Legend says that he drove all the snakes out of Ireland, although historians believe this is a metaphor for the disappearance of pagan beliefs.

It is also said that he used the three-leaf shamrock to explain the concept of the Holy Trinity to the Irish people. That’s why the shamrock is one of the main symbols of this festivity.

End of the second day:

Another day completed. We were amazed by the parade, the military band, the groups from different cultures with their costumes, and above all, the strong drinking culture that exists here. It was a unique experience and our first real encounter with Irish identity, one we will never forget.
